12 Laplace Transform

The Laplace transform is a solution method most suited in solving equations with discontinuous or impulsive forcing terms.
The Laplace transform is one of a series of tools called integral transforms. These are relations of the form
f(s)= ∫ α β K( s,t )f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t
where K( s,t ) is a given function, called the kernel of the transformation, and the limits of integration α and β are also given (though they may be ∞ and - ∞ ). The relation transforms the function f into F , or the transform of f .
From Calculus, we know an improper integral is defined as the limit of all integrals of finite intervals over an unbounded interval. If an integral from a to A exists for each A>a , and the limit of A → ∞ , the improper integral is said to converge to that limiting value; or otherwise, it diverges (or fails to exist).
∫ a ∞ f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t = lim A → ∞ ∫ a A f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t
The Laplace transform is defined as follows: where f(t) is given for t ≥ 0 and f satisfied certain conditions , the Laplace transform is defined by
L { f(t) }=F(s)= ∫ 0 ∞ e -st f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t
whenever this improper integral converges.
As solutions of linear differential equations with constant coefficients are based on the exponential function, the Laplace transform is useful for these equations.
Strategy to using the Laplace transform:
  1. Use the relation to transform an IVP for an unknown function f in the t -domain into the simpler, algebraic problem F in the s -domain.
  2. Solve the algebraic problem to find F .
  3. Recover the desired function f from its transform F by “inverting the transform”.
Example:
f(t)=1,t ≥ 0
L {1}= ∫ 0 ∞ e -st ⁢ ⅆ t =- lim A → ∞ . e -st s | 0 A = 1 s ,s>0
Another property of Laplace transforms: if f is a piecewise continuous function for t ≥ a , if | f(t) | ≤ g(t) when t ≥ M for some positive constant M , and if ∫ M ∞ g(t) ⁢ ⅆ t converges, then ∫ a ∞ f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t also converges. On the other hand, if f(t) ≥ g(t) ≥ 0 for t ≥ M , and if ∫ M ∞ g(t) ⁢ ⅆ t diverges, then ∫ a ∞ f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t also diverges.
Example:
f(t)={ 1, 0 ≤ t<1 k, t=1 0, t>1
Where k is a constant. f(t) often refers in an engineering context to a unit pulse (perhaps of force or voltage). Note that f is a piecewise continuous function.
L { f(t) }= ∫ 0 ∞ e -st f(t) ⁢ ⅆ t = ∫ 0 1 e -st ⁢ ⅆ t =- . e -st s | 0 1 = 1- e -s s ,s>0
Note that L { f(t) } does not depend on k , allowing several functions to have the same Laplace transform although differing in their value at a single point.
